Proposed Dividend
Proposed dividend is proposed by the Board of Directors and declared
(approved) by the shareholders in their Annual General Meeting. Board of
Directors propose the dividend after the annual accounts for the year have
been prepared. Annual General Meeting of the shareholders is held thereafter
meaning it is held in the next financial year.
Shareholders may reduce the amount of proposed dividend but cannot increase
it. Since declaration of proposed (final) dividend is contingent upon
shareholders approval, Proposed dividend is shown as contingent liability.
AS-4, Contingencies and Events Occurring after the Balance Sheet Date

AS-4, Contingencies and Events Occurring after the Balance Sheet Date

After the Proposed dividend is declared by the shareholders, it becomes a
liability for the company and is accounted in the books. As a consequence,
proposed dividend of previous year will be declared (approved) by the
shareholders in the current year and this declared (approved) proposed dividend
will be accounted during the year. Proposed dividend for the current year will
be relevant for the next financial year.
Briefly, proposed dividend of previous year will be accounted in the current
year after it is declared (approved) by the shareholders in their annual general
meeting.

  [HARD] [definition] Q1:
  Before shareholders approve a dividend, how is the proposed dividend classified in the financial statements according to the context provided?

    A) Liability
    B) Asset
    C) Equity
    D) Contingent liability
  ✗ Incorrect.
  Correct answer: D) Contingent liability
  Why: Proposed dividend is a contingent liability until shareholders approve it.

  [HARD] [scenario-based] Q2:
  A board proposes a dividend of Rs.10 per share, but shareholders approve only Rs.8 per share at the AGM. How should the company account for the proposed dividend immediately after the board’s proposal but before the AGM?

    A) Recognise Rs.10 per share as a liability immediately
    B) Recognise Rs.8 per share as a liability after the AGM
    C) Recognise Rs.10 per share as a contingent liability until the AGM
    D) Recognise Rs.8 per share as a contingent liability until the AGM
  ✗ Incorrect.
  Correct answer: C) Recognise Rs.10 per share as a contingent liability until the AGM
  Why: Proposed dividend is shown as a contingent liability until shareholders approve it.

  [HARD] [compare/contrast] Q3:
  Which statement correctly compares the accounting treatment of a proposed dividend and a declared dividend?

    A) Both are recorded as liabilities immediately
    B) Proposed dividend is a contingent liability; declared dividend is a liability
    C) Proposed dividend is an equity item; declared dividend is a liability
    D) Proposed dividend is an asset; declared dividend is a liability
  ✗ Incorrect.
  Correct answer: B) Proposed dividend is a contingent liability; declared dividend is a liability
  Why: Proposed dividend is a contingent liability; once declared it becomes a liability.

  [MEDIUM] [true-or-false-as-MCQ] Q4:
  Shareholders may increase the amount of a proposed dividend during the Annual General Meeting.

    A) True
    B) False
    C) Only if the board approves
    D) Only if the company has surplus
  ✗ Incorrect.
  Correct answer: B) False
  Why: Shareholders may reduce but cannot increase the proposed dividend.

  [HARD] [conceptual] Q5:
  Which accounting standard prescribes that a proposed dividend be shown in the Notes to Accounts?

    A) AS-1
    B) AS-3
    C) AS-4
    D) AS-7
  ✗ Incorrect.
  Correct answer: C) AS-4
  Why: AS-4, Contingencies and Events Occurring after the Balance Sheet Date, requires this disclosure.
